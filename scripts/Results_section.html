Results

Overview and Uncertainty

Point count data for 12 forest bird species on Montserrat were sampled in 13 years between 2011 and 2024, compromising 39120 observations from 3260 surveys.

We found evidence for elevational range changes in 5 out of the 12 analysed species (Figure 1, Table 1), with 3 species (Bridled Quail-Dove, Scaly-Breasted Thrasher and Brown Trembler) best explained by the ‘contraction’ model where only extinction depended on elevation, 1 species (Purple-Throated Carib) where colonisation was explained by elevation (‘expansion’ model), and 1 species (Montserrat Oriole) where both colonisation and extinction were explained by elevation (‘shift’ model). For another 4 species (Banaquit, Forest Thrush, Pearly-Eyed Thrasher and Scaly-Naped Pigeon) the most parsimonious model was the ‘constant’ model where both colonisation and extinction were modeled with intercepts only, and for 3 species (Antillean Crested Hummingbird, Caribbean Eleania and Green-Throated Carib) both extinction and colonisation were best explained by an annual trend (‘year-num’ model). An appropriate model fit and thus the validity of the models for inference was not questioned by the performed Chi-Squared goodness of fit test (n = 1000 simulations) since all estimted p-values were above the arbitrary threshold of 0.05 indicating no difference between the observed and survey data.

We found considerable model selection uncertainty in 5 species including 2 (Bridled-Quail Dove and Purple-Throated Carib) which exhibited elevational range changes. However, for these 2 species, the uncertainty only included whether elevation affected colonisation, extinction, or both (cumulative wAICc for all elevation model ≥0.5, Table S2), whereas models without elevation received less support (Table S2). In 2 other species (Forest Thrush and Scaly-Naped Pigeon), elevation was contained in a competitive, but not the most parsimonious model (Table S2).

Dynamics of colonisation and extinction along elevation [old]

For the Montserrat Oriole colonisation increased with elevation from 0.12563 to 0.98642 and extinction decreased with elevation from 0.15151 to 0.00387 (Table 3, Figure 2). Notably, the steepness of both colonisation and extinction slopes was highest at low elevations but flattening towards higher elevations, while at high elevations colonisation approached 1 and extinction approached 0. The ‘expansion’ model for the Purple-Throated Carib suggested that colonisation increased with elevation from 0.34107 and 0.97275 (Table 3). The models for the Brown Trembler and Bridled Quail-Dove indicated decreasing extinction along elevation ranging from 0.37957 to 0.00008 and 0.16656 to 0.00221, respectively. Again, extinction flattened considerably towards higher elevations. Contrastingly, for the Scaly-Breasted Thrasher extinction generally increased with elevation spanning from 0 to 0.16573.
